Velocity OS: The DNA of an Accelerating Biotech

By Johan Strömquist

A couple of years ago, I saw something that made me rethink everything about organizational speed.

It wasn't the 6.8-year development timeline (versus the industry's 10.5). It wasn't the real-time dashboard.

It was the parking lot at 5:30 PM. Half empty.

"We used to pride ourselves on late nights," the CEO said. "Then we realized: If everyone's here at 9 PM, we've designed our organization wrong."

The Architecture of Waiting

Here's what's actually happening in those late nights at most biotechs:

A clinical operations manager needs approval for a protocol amendment. Email at 4 PM. Director's in meetings until 6. Reads it at 7, has questions. Manager's gone home (kids). Responds at 9 PM. Director needs regulatory input. That's tomorrow. Regulatory needs medical. That's next Tuesday's meeting.

Two weeks later: approved. Actual review time? 90 minutes. Time elapsed? 336 hours.

This isn't a people problem. It's an architecture problem.

The GSK Revelation

GSK discovered they could run queries in 30 minutes that previously took a year. Their reaction wasn't celebration. It was horror.

If something taking a year could be done in 30 minutes, that's 99.97% waste. What else in their organization was running at a fraction of its potential?

The answer changed everything.

Their organization wasn't slow because the work was hard. It was slow because work spent most of its time waiting. Waiting for meetings. Waiting for approvals. Waiting for information. Waiting for alignment.

The Moderna Moment

When Chinese scientists released the genetic sequence of SARS-CoV-2 on January 11, 2020, Moderna didn't convene a committee. They didn't request a feasibility study.

They designed their vaccine.

In most biotechs, this moment would trigger months of analysis and debate. At Moderna...

Two days later, the company and the NIH designed the sequence for its coronavirus vaccine, called the mRNA-1273. The entire vaccine blueprint—the one that would eventually protect millions—was completed in 48 hours.

But here's what most miss: This wasn't heroic improvisation. This was the system working as designed. "We call mRNA the software of life," Bancel said. "You can copy and paste the information into a lot of drugs by using the same technology". Moderna had spent a decade building a platform where designing a vaccine was like updating code—plug in the new genetic sequence, and the platform does the rest.

When the moment came, they didn't need to be fast. They already were.

The Physics of Organizational Speed

Every organization has a clock speed - not how fast people move, but how quickly decisions flow. Most biotechs run at committee speed: weekly cycles, monthly reviews. The fastest run at continuous speed: decisions flow like data packets, not cargo ships.

Think of it like a highway. You can have the fastest cars in the world, but if there's a toll booth every mile, traffic crawls. Most biotechs are all toll booths.

The fastest biotechs do three things:

They make decisions flow downhill

In traditional biotechs, decisions climb org charts like salmon swimming upstream. A protocol change starts with a clinical manager, escalates to a director, waits for committee review, needs VP sign-off, requires cross-functional alignment. Each level adds a week. Each handoff adds uncertainty.

The fastest biotechs invert this. They push decision authority to the point of information. The person closest to the data has the clearest view—and the authority to act on it. Pre-defined criteria replace committee debates. If the data meets the threshold, the decision is already made. If it doesn't, that's clear too. No escalation needed. No interpretation required. The decision flows as naturally as water finding its level.

They treat information like a public good, not a private asset

In most biotechs, critical data lives in departmental silos, personal spreadsheets, email chains. In a Velocity OS, that same data lives in shared platforms. Not because sharing is virtuous, but because hoarding is slow.

When everyone can see the constraint, everyone can help solve it. When data is trapped in silos, problems hide until they explode.

They shrink the distance between learning and adjusting

Traditional: Run trial → Analyze data (months) → Design next trial → Get approval → Start recruiting. Total: 18 months.

Velocity: Adaptive trial design. Rolling analysis. Pre-approved modifications. Next cohort starts while current one ends. Total: 6 months.

Same rigor. Different rhythm.

The Compliance Excuse

"But we're regulated," they say. So is Moderna. So is BioNTech. Compliance doesn't require committees. It requires clarity.

The fastest biotechs aren't less compliant - they've just separated compliance from consensus. They've learned that you can meet every regulatory requirement without turning every decision into a town hall meeting.

The Incentive Problem

In most biotechs, the incentive structure actively rewards slow:

  • Get promoted for managing larger teams, not faster outcomes
  • Get recognized for running important committees, not eliminating them
  • Get valued for being the go-to person with critical information, not making it accessible
  • Get praised for catching problems in review, not preventing them upstream

These incentives persist because they're easy to measure. Headcount is visible. Committee membership is countable. Being "critical path" shows up on org charts. Speed doesn't have a line item.

This isn't malicious. These are good people responding to what gets rewarded. In a slow organization, being a bottleneck is job security. Being "critical path" is career advancement.

But what if we rewarded different things?

In a Velocity OS, the incentives flip:

  • Promoted for reducing cycle time, not increasing headcount
  • Recognized for decisions that don't need committees, not committees that make decisions
  • Valued for making information flow freely, not controlling its distribution
  • Praised for designing problems out, not heroically solving them

The same people, given different incentives, become accelerators instead of brakes. The middle manager who hoarded approvals now gets rewarded for delegating them. The committee chair who loved long meetings now gets recognized for making them unnecessary.

The fastest biotechs don't have better people. They have better incentives.

What Breaking Through Feels Like

I've watched organizations make this transition. The pattern is always the same:

Week one: Panic. "This will never work." Email chains explode with edge cases.

Week four: Something cracks. A two-week approval happens in two days. Not because standards dropped. Because waiting disappeared.

Week twelve: The parking lot starts emptying at reasonable hours. Not because people care less. Because work flows instead of pooling.

Six months: Timeline compression of 30-40%. Not through heroics. Through architecture.

The Question That Changes Everything

What would have to be true for your organization to move 40% faster without working harder?

Not more hours. Not more pressure. Not more people.

What would have to be true about how decisions flow? How information moves? How quickly you learn and adapt?

The fastest biotechs have discovered something profound: Speed isn't about working harder. It's about needing less work.

When information flows freely, you don't need meetings to share it. When decisions have clear owners, you don't need committees to make them. When feedback is continuous, you don't need quarterly reviews to course-correct.

Your Monday Morning Choice

Option 1: Add more pressure. Demand more urgency. Schedule more check-ins. Watch your timelines get longer while everyone gets more frustrated.

Option 2: Pick one decision that takes two weeks. Map where it spends its time. Remove the waiting. Make it take two days.

In 90 days, Option 1 burns out your best people. Option 2 shows them a different way is possible.

The operating system can be upgraded. But first, you have to admit your current one is broken.

Is your parking lot full at 9 PM because your people are dedicated? Or because your organization is broken?
