The CEO's office at 6:47 AM. Coffee going cold. The Rock Implementation Dashboard glowing accusingly on the screen.

Forty-five days into the protocol amendment transformation. Halfway through the 90-day sprint. And nothing was going according to plan.

The timeline was slipping. Two key team members had been pulled back to their day jobs "just for a critical issue." The pilot results were good but not spectacular. The Medical Review Committee was digging in harder than expected. And yesterday, Sarah—the Rock Owner who'd been so energized at launch—had sent an email asking for "a conversation about scope."

The CEO stared at the dashboard. Red indicators where green ones should be. Question marks where certainties had lived six weeks ago.

This is the Messy Middle. Every transformation has one. Most don't survive it.

Why the Middle Is Always Messy

William Bridges, in his seminal work on organizational transitions, made a crucial distinction that most leaders miss: change is what happens to the organization, transition is what happens to the people.

Change happens at a specific moment—you launch the Rock, you implement the new process, you announce the reorganization. Transition is the psychological journey people make as they slowly let go of the old way, navigate what Bridges called the "neutral zone," and eventually embrace the new beginning.

The Messy Middle is that neutral zone. And it's supposed to be uncomfortable.

At Day 45, five things are happening simultaneously:

1. The old structures are gone but the new ones aren't fully functional yet

You've dismantled the 14-approval protocol amendment process, but the new streamlined system still has bugs. People know the old way doesn't work anymore, but they're not yet fluent in the new way.

2. People are grieving what they've lost

Even when the old way was painful, it was familiar. The Medical Review Committee that never rejected anything still gave people a sense of thoroughness, of proper procedure. Taking that away creates loss, even when the replacement is objectively better.

3. Competence has temporarily decreased

Your team was expert at the old process. Now they're learning a new system, making mistakes, moving slower than they know they could. That feels terrible, especially for high performers.

4. The skeptics are getting louder

At Day 1, they were quiet, waiting to see if this was serious. By Day 45, when problems emerge, they're ready with "I told you so."

5. The early wins have worn off and the big wins aren't visible yet

The quick improvements you made in Week 2 felt exciting. By Week 7, they're just the new normal. The transformative outcomes you promised won't be fully visible until Week 11 or 12.

This is normal. This is the pattern of every transformation that actually succeeded.

According to McKinsey's research on organizational transformations, approximately 70% of change initiatives fail to achieve their goals. The primary causes? Inadequate management support (33%) and employee resistance (39%) during the middle phases of implementation. The organizations that succeed are those that maintain commitment precisely when uncertainty peaks.

Leading When You Don't Have Answers

The leadership challenge of the Messy Middle is unique: you need to project confidence in the destination while acknowledging you're not entirely sure about the path.

Most leaders default to one of two dysfunctional patterns:

False Certainty: "Everything is going exactly according to plan." Your team knows this is bullshit. They're living the chaos. When you pretend it's not happening, they conclude either that you're delusional or that you don't care about their reality.

Panic Transparency: "I don't know what we're doing. This might not work. Maybe we should rethink everything." This destroys the confidence people need to keep moving forward.

There's a third way: honest uncertainty.

In practice, it sounds like this:

"We're at Day 45, which means we're in the hardest part of this transformation. The old process is gone, the new one isn't fully working yet, and that's creating real friction. This is exactly what I expected to happen at this stage, and it still sucks.

What I know: The current state is unacceptable. The physics of why the old process was slow haven't changed—it was still 95% waiting. And the pilot results prove that sub-48-hour amendments are possible without sacrificing quality.

What I don't know: Exactly how we're going to solve the Medical Review Committee resistance. Whether we'll hit the full target in 90 days or need to extend the timeline.

What I believe: We're going to figure this out. Not because I have all the answers, but because this team has already done impossible things in the last 45 days.

What I need from you: Keep moving. When you hit obstacles, document them so we can solve them, but don't stop. Bring me the hard truths, not what you think I want to hear.

What you can count on from me: I will remove every obstacle I can. I will protect you from organizational antibodies. I will make decisions quickly when you need them. And I will not abandon this Rock before Day 90, no matter how messy it gets.

This is honest uncertainty. It acknowledges reality without surrendering to it. It admits what you don't know while being clear about what you do know.

Your team doesn't need you to have all the answers. They need you to hold the vision while they navigate the chaos.

The Temptation to Retreat

Somewhere around Day 50-60, you'll face the siren call of strategic retreat.

It sounds reasonable: "We've made good progress. Let's lock in these gains and call it a success. We can always do another Rock later to go further."

This is almost always a mistake.

The value of transformation is non-linear. Going from 42 days to 28 days is nice. Going from 42 days to 2 days is transformative. The first creates marginal improvement. The second changes how your organization functions.

If you retreat at Day 50 with partial progress, you've endured all the pain of the Messy Middle without capturing the exponential value on the other side. You've burned organizational change capital on incremental gains.

Worse, you've taught your organization that when transformation gets hard, you quit. That makes the next Rock dramatically harder to launch.

Push through when:

  • The problems you're facing are implementation challenges, not fundamental flaws in the design
  • The pilot results show the concept works, even if scaling is hard
  • The resistance is political ("we've always done it this way") not technical ("this violates regulations")
  • Your team is tired but not broken—they're still showing up, still problem-solving, still believing in the destination
  • The obstacles are specific and solvable ("need better integration between systems") not vague and endless ("nothing is working")

Pivot when:

  • The original design had a fatal assumption that's proven false
  • The target was based on incomplete information and the real target should be different
  • You've learned something that changes the problem definition
  • The team composition is wrong and different skills are needed

Stop when:

  • The regulatory or compliance assumption was wrong and this approach is actually risky
  • The organization is actively sabotaging the Rock in ways you can't overcome
  • You've learned that this problem isn't actually the priority and organizational energy should go elsewhere

The Messy Middle Playbook

Six specific practices help leaders and teams navigate Days 45-75:

1. The Weekly Pulse Check

Every Friday, three questions for your Rock Owner and team:

  • On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you we'll hit the target?
  • What's the single biggest obstacle right now?
  • What do you need from me to keep moving?

Track confidence over time. If it's dropping week over week, that's a signal. If it's holding steady or fluctuating but not collapsing, that's normal Messy Middle turbulence.

2. The Obstacle Elimination Protocol

When obstacles surface, categorize them:

  • Can solve immediately (you have authority and resources): Solve it now, that day.
  • Can solve with effort (requires negotiation, resources, decisions): Assign an owner and deadline.
  • Can't solve (regulatory requirement, physics, external dependency): Acknowledge it and design around it.
  • Shouldn't solve (team needs to work through it themselves): Provide guidance but let them own the solution.

3. The Resilience Rhythm

The Messy Middle is a marathon in the middle of a sprint. You need sustainable intensity, not burnout heroics.

  • No weekend work (except true emergencies): People need recovery time to sustain 90-day intensity
  • Protected time for deep work: The Messy Middle creates noise. Protect focus time for actual building.
  • Visible progress markers: Create a physical or digital representation of progress that the team can see

4. The Honest Retrospective

Every two weeks, 45 minutes:

  • What's working better than expected?
  • What's working worse than expected?
  • What have we learned that changes our approach?
  • What do we need to stop doing?
  • What do we need to start doing?

Make this psychologically safe. If people only report successes, you're not getting the truth.

5. The Sponsor Check-In

If you're the Rock Owner, you need 15 minutes weekly with your executive sponsor.

Not a status report. A real conversation:

  • "Here's where I'm stuck."
  • "Here's where I need top-cover."
  • "Here's what's keeping me up at night."
  • "Here's what I need from you."

6. The Team Shield

The Messy Middle attracts critics. Every problem, every delay, every misstep will be weaponized by people who didn't want this transformation in the first place.

Your job as leader is to shield your team from organizational politics so they can focus on the work.

Your team should spend 90% of their energy on the transformation, 10% on managing stakeholders. If those percentages flip, you're not shielding effectively.

What Day 60 Looks Like (If You Do This Right)

If you navigate the Messy Middle well, here's what starts to happen around Day 60:

  • The solution starts to work: Not perfectly, but consistently. The bugs get ironed out. People start to develop fluency with the new way.
  • Early adopters emerge: A few people start preferring the new process to the old one. They become your proof points and advocates.
  • The team finds its rhythm: The chaos starts to feel more like controlled chaos. The panic of "we're never going to make it" shifts to "this is hard but we're going to figure it out."
  • You see the finish line: It's still 30 days away, but you can see the path. The obstacles are specific and solvable.
  • The skeptics get quieter: When they realize you're not quitting, when they see the solution actually working, the active resistance starts to fade.

The Truth About Leadership in Transition

The Messy Middle is where real leadership happens.

Anyone can lead when things are going well. The vision is clear, the path is obvious, the team is energized, the results are flowing. That's not leadership, that's stewardship.

Anyone can lead in a crisis. The urgency is obvious, the priorities are clear, the organization aligns naturally. Crisis leadership is intense but straightforward.

The Messy Middle is different. The urgency has faded but the work is harder than ever. The vision is still clear but the path is obscured. The team is exhausted but the finish line isn't yet visible. The early wins are gone and the big wins aren't here yet.

This is where most transformations die. Not from active failure, but from passive abandonment. Leaders lose faith, teams lose energy, organizations lose patience.

If you can lead through this—if you can hold the vision while acknowledging the chaos, maintain confidence while admitting uncertainty, support your team while managing your own doubt—you're doing the work that actually transforms organizations.

Research from the Journal of Business Strategy shows that successful transformations capture approximately half their value in the first 18 months, with the remaining half coming after that point. But only if leaders maintain commitment through the middle phase.

At Day 52 in one protocol amendment transformation, the CEO called me at 11 PM. "This isn't working," he said. "We should declare victory on the progress we've made and move on."

I asked him what specifically wasn't working.

Long pause. "Nothing is catastrophically broken. We're just not going to hit the target. And I'm tired of fighting for this."

"The physics hasn't changed," I told him. "Forty-two days was still 95% waiting. The solution design is still sound. The pilot results still prove it works. The only question is whether you're willing to push through the next 38 days."

Another long pause. "I don't know if I am."

"Then don't decide tonight. Sleep on it. Look at the data tomorrow. Talk to Sarah and see if she wants to quit. If she does, we'll shut it down. If she doesn't, give her the 38 days."

He did. Sarah didn't want to quit. They pushed through.

Ninety days later, they were processing protocol amendments in 52 hours. Six months later, 24 hours. The transformation stuck.

But it almost didn't. Not because the approach was wrong. Because the Messy Middle almost won.

Don't let it win for you.

The work you're doing is hard. It's supposed to be hard. If it were easy, someone would have done it already.

The doubt you're feeling is normal. The exhaustion is real. The uncertainty is unavoidable.

And none of it means you should quit.

Forty-five more days. That's all the Messy Middle gets. Not one day more.

On the other side: transformation.
