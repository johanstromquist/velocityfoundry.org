Just the other day I saw that Anthropic has released some new tools for Claude that connect it to public life science data sources—making Claude smarter at biological and medical research. A colleague pointed me to the news. Thank you, Ingela.

My first thought wasn't cool, let's check it out.

It was cool, I should be able to do that too.

The MMAgent

I'm not sure I'm allowed to say this, but before Christmas the team at Metalmind—our boutique GenAI agency—had created the MMAgent: a lightweight framework for integrating agents into host applications.

Since then they've built a proof of concept. A generalist agent that can be equipped with different skills. I loved the experience of working with it. Super easy to use, very helpful, and an incredibly cool use of tools.

The greatest advantage of the MMAgent is the ease with which you can add new skills to it. So considering Anthropic's play, I figured—why not try to create similar tools for our agent?

Two Hours

It took two hours with Claude Code.

Then I had the first seven connectors in place:

  • PubMed — 35 million+ biomedical articles
  • bioRxiv + medRxiv — preprints from the life sciences
  • ChEMBL — 2.4 million+ bioactive molecules
  • UniProt — protein sequences and functional information
  • Open Targets — drug-target associations
  • Reactome — biological pathways
  • ClinicalTrials.gov — 500,000+ registered clinical trials

It took another five minutes and the agent had written a desktop research piece on the current status of Trastuzumab in HER2-positive breast cancer.

Five minutes.

The Trajectory Problem

I wrote The Velocity Framework naive to the practical impact of agents on knowledge work, but very much prepared for the capabilities they offer.

It is challenging to look at your current situation and try to draw out a trajectory of development. We're kind of forever stuck in a world where things are the way they are. And after the next change we shrug and still believe they are the way they are.

Dragging out that trajectory from yesteryear's AI capabilities to where we are today is easy. But continuing to extend it meaningfully into the future is incredibly difficult.

I do believe that working with the latest the labs have to offer gives you a continual glimpse of the future and what is to come.

The Auto-Coder Vision

When I wrote The Velocity Framework I was 100% convinced that knowledge workers will solve a large amount of their own day-to-day assignments using code.

They will build solutions—or speak them into existence—to make the problem go away forever.

What's left is going to be a new frontier of impossibilities. Things we never imagined we could do, simply because we couldn't, that we are now suddenly capable of.

You want to build an agent? Ask your auto-coder.

You want to build an immersive online role-playing game? Ask your auto-coder.

You want to write a book? Ask your auto-coder.

What This Means for Biotechs

For biotechs this is good news.

It's only good news, and not great, because we still have to change. We have to embrace the new opportunities. We have to learn. We have to experiment, fail, and be frustrated.

Those who do this fast will be ahead, for sure.

But more than that—they will be the ones making the greatest difference to the patients they are trying to serve.
