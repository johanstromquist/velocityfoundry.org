In late April 2025, on a quarterly earnings call, the CEO of Starbucks told investors that a specific bet had failed. For the previous two years, the company had been removing labor from its stores. The reasoning was straightforward: a new drink-making system, a cold brew machine, food-heating equipment -- all of it should have allowed fewer baristas to move the same volume of orders. Margins were thin. Equipment was the lever.

It had not worked. Sales were down for the fifth consecutive quarter. Brian Niccol explained the reason. "Equipment doesn't solve the customer experience that we need to provide," he said. "Staffing the stores and deploying with this technology behind it does." The drink-making system was being scaled back. A staffing pilot -- more baristas, more hours -- was being expanded to around three thousand of the company's thirty-six thousand stores worldwide. Handwritten notes on cups were back. Ceramic cups were back. The seating was being attended to. When these details returned, customers, in Niccol's phrasing, were sitting and staying.

The lesson was narrower than the press headlines made it sound. Starbucks had not concluded that automation in general had failed; mobile ordering, the app, the back-end logistics were untouched. What had failed was the specific bet that the human-shaped portion of the encounter could be done with fewer humans and faster machines. The customer was not paying for throughput. The customer was paying for the part of the encounter where a person showed up.

One company found this out for itself. The same question is now arriving for the rest of the economy.

The Bigger Question

When AI makes commodity production cheap -- when the things become inexpensive to produce in volume, with high quality, with little human labor -- what stays valuable in the spaces left behind?

Most accounts of AI and work answer with some version of "less and less." Half of entry-level white-collar jobs gone in five years. Mass displacement. Ghost GDP. Grim arithmetic.

A recent essay by Alex Imas, an economist at Chicago Booth, asks the question directly and arrives at a different answer. Imas opens, as it happens, with the same Niccol earnings call. He reads it as a canary -- if anywhere should have been first to mechanise, it was a global chain selling a heavily standardised product, and yet the company found itself reversing course. From there, Imas builds a model in which falling commodity prices shift demand and employment toward what he calls the relational sector: the part of the economy where the human aspect is part of the value. Care, hospitality, education, the arts, therapy, mentorship, attention-intensive work. By his calibration, the sector grows from roughly 30% of the economy to over 40% under a doubling of automated productivity. Jobs shift even faster, because the relational sector is more labor-intensive than the commodity sector it absorbs from.

This is, by the standards of recent commentary, an optimistic prediction. It says that the technology displacing labor from one sector is also enlarging the demand for labor in another. The economy is not becoming jobless; it is reorganising.

The question is what is doing the work.

A Real Mechanism, A Specific Scope

Imas grounds his prediction in what economists, following René Girard, call mimetic desire. The basic idea: we want what others want, and we want it most when others can't have it. As people get richer and basic needs are met, this comparative dimension of desire grows in importance. Goods whose value depends on others not having them -- exclusive, scarce, unmistakably non-mass-produced -- become a larger share of what the wealthy consume. Human-made goods, in this framing, retain a perception of genuine scarcity that AI-made goods cannot replicate. The premium people pay for the human element is, on this account, a status premium.

The evidence is substantial. In a series of behavioural experiments with the LSE economist Kristóf Madarász, willingness to pay for a good roughly doubled when subjects learned that a random subset of others would be excluded from access to it -- not for status signalling (the experiments were anonymous), not for a scarcity heuristic (the exclusion was random), but as a pure preference for having something others did not. In a more recent study with Graelin Mandel, human-made artwork gained 44% in value from being declared one-of-one rather than mass-produced; AI-generated artwork gained only 21%. The mere involvement of AI made the work feel inherently reproducible. Provenance -- the traceability of the human in the loop -- was the load-bearing element.

The mechanism is real. It is documented. And for a specific class of goods -- fine art, luxury craft, designer fashion, signed objects, the things people buy as positional assets -- it is almost certainly the right account. Hand-stitched suits exist because some buyers want what a machine cannot offer them: the assurance that a human made it, and that not everyone has one.

The question is whether this same mechanism explains the rest of the relational sector.

The Extension That Quietly Does the Work

Imas extends his account broadly. The mimetic-exclusivity engine, he writes, applies to "teachers, nurses, therapists, childcare workers, trainers, hospitality, clergy, guides, and many forms of local services. In all of these cases the human being is not just an input into the production process. Their judgment, attention, memory, warmth, or presence is an integral part of the value."

The first sentence of that passage is the extension. The second is the definition of belonging.

These are different things. The premium a buyer pays for an Armani jacket runs on exclusivity -- what makes it valuable is that not everyone has it. The premium a parent pays for an attentive teacher does not, on any natural reading, run on this. Parents do not want their child's teacher to be exclusive. They want their child to be known, to be attended to, to be one of a small enough group that the teacher can hold the whole shape of who that child is becoming. They want the teacher to be present. The good they are buying is recognition, not exclusion.

The same is true for a nurse, a therapist, a hospice worker, a coach, a community organiser, a hairdresser who has cut your hair for fifteen years. The reason these relationships carry value is not that machines could not replicate them -- though that is true -- and not that other people are excluded from having them -- they are not. The reason is that they are participations. Both parties are present. Both parties become more themselves through the encounter. The nurse who knows your medication history matters to you because you matter to her, and the loop closes in a way no transaction quite captures.

This is a different engine. Call it belonging.

It is consistent with the same model. Imas's prediction holds either way: the relational sector grows, jobs shift toward it, expenditure follows. But which engine is doing the work matters enormously, because the two engines describe different societies.

The Fork

Return to Niccol's earnings call. He read his data and concluded that the human element was load-bearing. Suppose his company has to decide what to do with that conclusion. Two designs are available, and the same earnings call would justify either one.

If the engine is status, the move is to lean into the human as a premium good. Smaller stores. Higher prices. Reservations. Personalised greetings backed by a customer database. A loyalty programme tiered by spend. Eventually a bifurcated brand -- a premium tier with the human touch, a discount tier without. Other premium service brands are doing exactly this, and the playbook is well understood. The barista becomes a kind of butler, professionalised and rewarded for the exclusivity of the encounter. The customer pays for an experience that depends, at the margins, on others not having it.

If the engine is belonging, the move is to make the store something people are inside, not something they are sold. The barista is hired to stay, paid enough to stay, and given enough authority over her store that it feels like hers. The regulars are known by name and by drink. The store is sized and laid out to feel like a public room of the neighborhood, somewhere customers come because the company there is the point. The chain looks less like a single brand pushed outward from headquarters and more like a network of local rooms that share a name.

Both designs would be consistent with Niccol's conclusion. Both would produce a more human-shaped Starbucks. Neither follows from the data alone. The choice between them is a choice about which engine the company believes is doing the work.

The same fork waits at every institution the model says is growing. A school that finds its small classes are valued can build that into a premium private offering or into a public investment in lower student-teacher ratios for everyone. A clinic that finds patients value continuity can sell concierge subscriptions or build a permanent patient roster around the same staff. A platform that finds its users care about being among other users can wall the community off behind a paid tier or hand the rules of the room to the people inside it. Each fork looks, on the spreadsheet, like the same opportunity. Each leads somewhere different.

This is what is at stake in the engine question. Not whether the relational sector grows -- that part is, in the model's terms, going to happen -- but what kind of sector it becomes once it has grown. A sector organised around exclusion looks like a butler economy: human warmth as a tier, attention as a luxury, presence as a positional asset. A sector organised around participation looks like a fabric: the relationships are how the place holds itself together, and what you pay for is the right to be inside the weave rather than the right to be served from outside it.

The choice is not made all at once. It is made in thousands of design decisions taken one at a time, most of them without anyone naming the engine that is being picked. That is the danger of leaving the question implicit. Designs default toward the engine the surrounding culture treats as obvious, and our surrounding culture has spent a long time treating exclusivity as the natural form of premium value.

Naming the alternative is the first move. The relational sector the model predicts is not, by default, organised around belonging. Belonging is what we have to choose deliberately, against the grain of an inherited assumption that the human element is most valuable when it is most scarce.

The Choice the Model Doesn't Make

The cheaper the things get, the more the relationships matter. That much is going to be true regardless. What is up to us is whether mattering is mediated by exclusion or by participation. Whether the new economy is shaped, at its core, by status or by belonging.

We choose.
