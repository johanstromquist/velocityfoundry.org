The Velocity Foundry

By Johan Strömquist

← All Posts

A clinical operations director recently reflected over her "database" - an Excel file with 47 tabs, 12,000 rows, and a prayer that it wouldn't corrupt before the trial ended.

"We asked IT for a real system three years ago," she said. "They quoted us $200k and 18 months. Still waiting."

That same week, her daughter launched three web applications that she had built for her computer science class. In a month.

The director stared at her screen. "How is it that my teenager can build apps faster than our IT department?"

Welcome to biotech's uncomfortable truth: While we've been mastering gene therapy, the rest of the world made building software as accessible as building PowerPoint presentations. We just haven't noticed yet.

The New Reality of Software Creation

Let's be clear: Despite what AI vendors promise, you cannot speak complex applications into existence (at least not yet). Building useful software still requires understanding logic, data flow, and user needs.

But - and this is crucial - new AI powered auto-coders like Cursor, Claude Code, and OpenAI's Codex have brought the barrier to entry to the brink of collapse. These tools don't eliminate the need for knowledge. They amplify what you can do with basic knowledge.

Think of it like this: Excel didn't make everyone an accountant, but it made millions of people capable of financial modeling. Modern AI development tools won't make everyone a software engineer, but combined with the right platform, they're making domain experts capable of building their own solutions.

The question for biotech: How do we harness this capability without creating chaos?

The Platform Engineering Revolution

For years tech companies and startups have worked internally to solve one specific problem: How to let teams build tools without rebuilding infrastructure every time.

Netflix calls their solution "Paved Roads" - pre-built paths that handle security, deployment, and integration. Spotify calls theirs "Golden Paths". Same concept: Make the right way the easy way.

Golden paths eliminate the technical overhead that stops domain experts from building tools. Instead of wrestling with authentication, databases, and compliance, these become pre-solved problems. The foundational complexity disappears into the platform.

Here's what this means for biotech: That clinical operations director doesn't need to know how to implement 21 CFR Part 11 compliance. The platform handles it. She just needs to know what data her trial needs to track.

What Actually Becomes Possible

Let me show you what real biotech professionals are building:

Week 1-2: Excel Exodus

Convert that 47-tab monster into a simple web form that validates site IDs and protocol numbers automatically. Add dropdown menus for investigator names. Auto-calculate enrollment rates. Nothing fancy - just reliable.

Month 1-3: Workflow Intelligence

Build a sample chain-of-custody tracker that actually knows where your -80°C samples are. Create an amendment tracker that shows which sites have implemented version 2.1 versus 2.2. Design a training matrix that alerts you when certifications expire.

Month 6+: Integrated Solutions

Connect your plate reader directly to your data system. Build multi-step approval workflows for protocol deviations. Create real-time dashboards that pull from three different CROs simultaneously.

The key insight: Each level provides immediate value. Even the simplest tools eliminate error-prone manual processes.

The Economics of Incremental Improvement

There is also a beauty and underestimated power in addressing small, recurring issues. Forget the moonshot applications. Focus on the compound effect of small improvements.

That adverse event form that takes 20 minutes to complete manually? A basic builder can create a version that takes 5 minutes and validates against your protocol automatically. Multiply by every form, every workflow, every repeated task across your organization.

Your team will spend less than 1% of their time building an application. The remaining 99% is using it. A simple tool that saves 10 minutes daily delivers more value than a complex system that never gets adopted.

But What About...

"Validation and 21 CFR Part 11?" Built into the platform. Every application inherits audit trails, electronic signatures, and data integrity controls. You validate the platform once, not every application.

"Creating a mess of unsupported applications?" That's why you need golden paths. Unlike the Excel sprawl we have today, these applications share common infrastructure, follow consistent patterns, and are discoverable by design.

"How is this different from low-code platforms?" Low-code platforms force you into their worldview and eco system. This approach gives you real code - maintainable, portable, and unlimited - while removing the infrastructure burden.

The Path Forward

Building your organizational foundry isn't magic. It's methodical:

1. Create the Foundation

Adopt (don't build) baseline infrastructure for deploying internal applications. This handles authentication, security, logging, data storage, and compliance tracking. Partners matter here - building this yourself is like generating your own electricity instead of plugging into the grid.

2. Define Your Golden Paths

Identify workflows that cross multiple teams. Sample tracking. Protocol management. Adverse event reporting. These become templates - pre-built starting points any team can customize.

3. Start Small, Ship Fast

Build the simplest useful version of each path. A basic sample tracker that works beats a perfect one in development. You'll iterate based on actual use, not imagined requirements.

4. Enable Your Experts

Your Excel power users are your pioneers. Give them AI coding tools and focused training. Not "intro to programming" but "build a sample tracker in two hours." Make it practical, immediate, and relevant.

5. Evolve Through Use

Each tool built reveals what's missing from your platform. Each builder's feedback improves the paths. The platform grows smarter with every application.

The Reality Check

This transformation requires investment in both technology and people. Not everyone will become a builder, and not every problem needs a custom solution.

But in an industry where a single efficiency gain can accelerate drug development by months, where better data management can mean better patient outcomes, can you afford not to explore this capability?

The future isn't about biotech companies becoming software companies. It's about biotech professionals having the power to solve their own problems without waiting for IT, vendors, or budget cycles.

The forge can be built. The paths can be paved. And that clinical operations director with the 47-tab Excel file? She could be building her first real application next month.
