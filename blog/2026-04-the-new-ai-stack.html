There is a model running on my desk right now. It has twenty-six billion parameters, it fits in memory alongside everything else I am doing, and when I benchmarked it this week against an API model that costs money with every call, it matched on eighty-nine percent of the tasks I gave it. The eleven percent it missed were not failures of comprehension but of vocabulary -- it reached for a synonym where the scoring expected the precise term.

Six months ago, nothing local could have come close. A year before that, the capability this model offers would have cost hundreds of dollars an hour in API fees, if it was available at all. A year before that, it did not exist.

This is the trajectory that matters. Not which model won last week's benchmark. Not whether Anthropic or OpenAI shipped first. The trajectory: what required a datacenter yesterday runs on a desktop today and will run on a phone tomorrow. What cost a fortune becomes free. What was scarce becomes abundant. Intelligence flows downhill, and it is flowing faster than it ever has.

The Hunger

The reason it is flowing so fast is that the world just discovered what AI is actually for.

For three years, since ChatGPT made language models a household concept, the dominant interaction was the question. You asked; it answered. A single exchange, a few hundred tokens, a moment of someone's attention. Revenue scaled with the number of people asking.

Then agents arrived, and the economics changed entirely.

An agent does not ask a question. It takes a shift. Claude Code -- Anthropic's autonomous coding platform -- works a codebase for hours, making thousands of tool calls, consuming billions of tokens in a single session. Its launch demo built a C compiler from scratch, autonomously, for thousands of dollars in compute. OpenClaw, the open-source agent framework that became the most-starred project in GitHub history before its creator was hired by OpenAI, introduced primitives that every major platform rushed to adopt: persistent sessions, scheduled tasks, always-on execution. Anthropic shipped Cowork, Remote Control, Dispatch, and Channels. Perplexity, Notion, and Airtable launched their own agent platforms within weeks. Opus 4.6 and GPT 5.3 Codex dropped within fifteen minutes of each other, chasing the same insight -- that a great coding agent is a great work agent, full stop.

The numbers tell the rest. Claude Code's annualized revenue doubled in two months. Anthropic's overall run rate tripled in four -- from nine billion dollars at the end of 2025 to over thirty billion by April 2026. These are not growth numbers. They are phase-change numbers, the kind that appear when a technology shifts from being something people use to something that uses itself.

And so the tokens ran short. Usage caps tightened. Forums filled with posts about diminishing quotas. It felt like scarcity, and in the short term it was -- demand that the labs foresaw but could not build capacity to meet fast enough. The infrastructure will catch up. It always does. But in the meantime, the scarcity is doing what scarcity always does.

Creating pressure. And pressure is how intelligence flows downhill.

Gravity

Every dollar poured into a frontier model today creates the open-source model you will run for free tomorrow. This is not hope. It is the observed pattern, and it is accelerating.

The evidence sits in a single benchmark progression. SWE-bench measures whether a model can fix real bugs in real codebases -- not a toy test but genuine software engineering. In mid 2024, the best open model needed seventy billion parameters to score thirty percent. By early 2025, DeepSeek V3 reached fifty-five percent with thirty-seven billion active parameters. Late 2025, Kimi K2 hit sixty-six percent with thirty-two billion. In January 2026, Kimi K2.5 reached seventy-seven percent. And early this year, a Qwen coding model crossed seventy percent with three billion active parameters.

Three billion. The same score that required seventy billion parameters eighteen months earlier. Active parameters dropping by an order of magnitude per year while scores double. This is the sound of gravity working -- the accumulated weight of competition and investment pressing capability relentlessly down the cost curve.

The mechanism is not charity. DeepSeek built V3 to compete with GPT-4. Moonshot built Kimi to compete with Claude. Qwen built their coder to compete with everyone. Each generation of frontier investment produces the architecture innovations, the training breakthroughs, the distillation techniques that make the next generation of small models possible. The frontier does not just advance. It leaves behind the tools for others to catch up. And they catch up faster each time.

Now look at where the billions are going. Anthropic's Claude Mythos leaked through an unsecured database -- three thousand unreleased assets, a model Anthropic confirmed as "a step change in performance," a tier above Opus, not an increment. They are releasing it with the care of a company eyeing a Q4 IPO. OpenAI's Spud has completed pre-training. Codex, ChatGPT, and the Atlas browser are merging into a single platform. Both companies are building toward capabilities that do not yet exist: discovering drug candidates, proving mathematical theorems, generating weeks of continuous code for applications no one has been able to create.

Most people will never use these models for any of that. They will write haikus. They will sort their recipe collections. They will ask for gardening advice and be disappointed. "What was all the fuss?" they will say, because the frontier is always wasted on the ordinary, and the ordinary never understands why the frontier was necessary.

But the frontier does not exist for the haikus. It exists because the billions poured into Mythos and Spud will produce the architectures, the efficiencies, the open-weight descendants that -- in twelve to eighteen months -- run on consumer hardware at commodity prices. Mythos will be extraordinary. And Mythos will be the reason that models twice as capable as today's Opus sit on your desk by 2028, costing nothing per token, available to anyone.

The frontier funds the gravity. It always has. It is doing so now at a scale and speed without precedent.

The Flow

You do not have to wait. It is already here.

Google recently released Gemma 4 -- open-source, built from the same research as Gemini 3, the first Western models in years to compete with China's best open offerings. The twenty-six-billion-parameter variant runs on a Mac. What it does, at no cost, on hardware you already own, is roughly what people were paying API prices for eighteen months ago.

Cursor built its Composer 2 on Moonshot's Kimi K2.5, layered reinforcement learning on top, and produced a coding model that beats Opus 4.6 on its own benchmarks at a fraction of the cost. Intercom's Fin agent outperforms frontier models in customer service. Decagon moved the majority of its inference in-house. The API tax -- paying frontier prices for commodity work -- is starting to look like the cloud markup of a decade ago. Understandable at the time. Increasingly hard to justify.

And the cloud will not stand still. As the demand spike settles, as capacity catches up with appetite, inference costs will come down -- just as they have with every wave of cloud computing before this one. The destination is not local instead of cloud. It is cheap intelligence, everywhere. On your desk when that makes sense. In the cloud when it does not. Capability first, then scale, then price. The same three phases every infrastructure technology has followed. AI inference is following them now, on fast-forward.

Riding the Curve

I build on both sides of this divide, and I can tell you what it feels like to ride the curve.

My inference architecture splits work between Anthropic's API and a local model. The frontier handles reasoning -- the hard, multi-step, genuinely novel work that nothing local can touch. The local model handles volume -- the fast, repetitive calls that would burn through API quotas in hours. The arrangement keeps the engines running. When capacity is rationed, the work does not stop.

The benchmark I ran this week tells the story of where the curve is right now. Nine code-exploration scenarios. Three rounds each. Small projects up to a full game engine. Haiku -- Anthropic's fast API tier -- scored perfectly. The local Gemma 4 model hit eighty-nine percent, with the gap almost entirely in vocabulary: it said "route" where Haiku said "endpoint," "job" where Haiku said "session." Correct answers, slightly less precise language. And the local model is free, unlimited, running on hardware I already own. Six months ago, nothing local could have done this at all.

I have been designing dedicated inference hardware. The first plan was a six-GPU build -- expensive, power-hungry, dependent on driver software I did not trust. The revised plan is two Apple Silicon machines that provide three and a half times more model memory at less than half the cost. The workloads I am targeting -- coding agents, game AI, routing and classification -- get cheaper on local hardware every quarter as the models shrink and the silicon improves. By the time the machines arrive, they will be more capable than I need. That is the point.

The tools change. They have changed three times in the past two months. A communication layer I built and open-sourced was replaced the following week by something Anthropic shipped natively. A delegation system I rigged to preserve API quotas became unnecessary when local models got good enough. The specific infrastructure is disposable. The principle is not: maximum intelligence for minimum cost, and the flexibility to follow the curve wherever it leads next.

The Vector

The industry is having two conversations, and only one of them matters.

The loud conversation is about which model is best. Opus or Codex. Mythos or Spud. Benchmarks, leaderboards, launch-day theatrics. It is exciting, and it is ephemeral, because capability is converging. The distance between first and second place shrinks with every generation. In a year, the question of which model is best will feel as quaint as asking which cloud provider has the best virtual machine.

The quiet conversation is about the cost of intelligence. How to wring the most capability from the least expenditure. How to build systems supple enough to shift between providers, between cloud and local, between whatever comes next -- following the curve wherever it bends. This conversation is tedious, and in the long run it is the only one that changes anything. The efficiency curve does not stop. The gap between those who ride it and those who do not compounds silently, quarter after quarter, until it is too wide to cross.

I do not know what my infrastructure will look like in a year. I know the direction. More intelligence. Less cost. More choices about where and how to run it. That is the vector. Everything else -- every product name, every benchmark score, every breathless launch announcement -- is a snapshot on a curve that has not stopped moving since the first GPU was repurposed for matrix multiplication, and will not stop in our lifetimes.

The frontier you pay a premium for today will be cheap tomorrow. On your desk, in the cloud, or both. Intelligence is becoming infrastructure, and infrastructure -- given enough time, enough investment, enough pressure -- always becomes abundant.

Build for that.
