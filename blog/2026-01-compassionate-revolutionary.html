There's a particular kind of leader I keep encountering in organizational transformations. They're the ones who can sit in a meeting, see with perfect clarity that a process is wasting hundreds of hours every week, and simultaneously feel the weight of what change will cost the people who built their identities around that process.

Most leaders collapse into one side or the other.

Some become efficiency zealots—all metrics, no humanity. They see the waste and charge forward, leaving casualties in their wake. Others become empathy paralyzed—so sensitive to the human dimension that urgency dies in endless consensus-building.

The rare ones hold both. I call them compassionate revolutionaries.

The Collapse Points

I've watched brilliant leaders fail by collapsing into a single dimension.

There's the CTO who saw that their deployment process was fundamentally broken—eighteen approvals for a simple configuration change—and bulldozed through a new system in two weeks. Technically perfect. Organizationally devastating. Six months later, they couldn't understand why adoption was at 12% and passive resistance was everywhere.

Then there's the VP who spent nine months building consensus for a transformation everyone agreed was necessary. So many listening sessions. So much empathy. Meanwhile, the parking lot stayed full until 9 PM, marriages strained, burnout mounted. The human cost of not changing kept accumulating while they sought perfect alignment.

Both cared deeply. Both were smart. Both collapsed into a single dimension because holding the tension felt impossible.

What the Research Actually Shows

Here's what makes this interesting: the data doesn't support either extreme.

BCG's 2024 research shows that empathetic leadership directly increases psychological safety, which drives the ability to "innovate quickly, unlock the benefits of diversity, and adapt well to change." Empathy isn't soft—it's the mechanism that makes transformation stick.

But empathy without urgency fails spectacularly. Seventy percent of organizational change initiatives fail. Digital transformation success rates in traditional industries hover between 4-11%.

Meanwhile, organizations that excel at change management—what researchers call "change accelerators"—achieve 264% greater revenue growth than those with weak change practices.

The pattern is clear: organizations that integrate urgency and empathy vastly outperform those that choose between them.

The question is how.

The Actual Work

Let me tell you what this looks like in practice, because it's not what most leadership books describe.

You're in a meeting. You can see that the approval chain for infrastructure changes is absurd—seventeen people reviewing what one senior engineer should approve. You can calculate the cost: 336 hours of organizational time for 90 minutes of actual work.

You can also see that three of those seventeen reviewers have built their entire professional identity around being "the gatekeeper." Their sense of value, their political capital, their daily routine—all tied to a process that needs to die.

The efficiency zealot sees only the 336 wasted hours and moves to eliminate the process.

The empathy-paralyzed leader sees only the three people and can't pull the trigger.

The compassionate revolutionary sees both and acts anyway—but differently.

They eliminate the wasteful process because they care about people. Because organizational friction doesn't just waste time—it strains marriages, misses kids' bedtimes, creates chronic stress. The parking lot full at 9 PM represents human suffering that compounds daily.

But they also create transition paths for the gatekeepers. Not as a consolation prize for losing their fiefdom, but because those people have skills the organization needs—just deployed against different problems.

They move fast and they address the human dimension. Not as a tradeoff. As an integration.

The 3 AM Test

Here's how you know you're doing this work:

You wake at 3 AM, mind churning through both the transformation timeline and the transition support plan. You feel urgency pushing you forward and empathy pulling you to slow down, and neither voice is wrong.

You sit in transformation meetings where you see the solution with perfect clarity and also see the fear in people's eyes. You have to find words that honor both the physics and the humanity.

You make decisions that are right for the organization and hard on individuals. You create systems that reduce human toil and still grieve the identities built around that toil.

This is the actual work: holding the tension between urgency and empathy without collapsing into either extreme.

It's being both fierce and tender. Demanding and understanding. Clear-eyed and warm-hearted.

It's harder than either extreme because you can't escape into the comfort of a single story.

Why Both Are Compassionate

Here's the insight that changed how I think about this:

Moving slowly isn't more compassionate than moving fast. Letting dysfunction persist because change is uncomfortable isn't kindness—it's a different kind of cruelty.

When you see that a process wastes 95% of people's effort, leaving it in place because change is hard means you're choosing prolonged suffering over temporary disruption. You're being compassionate to the current dysfunction at the expense of everyone trapped in it.

The truly compassionate act is to move with urgency and support people through the transition.

To see the parking lot full at 9 PM and refuse to accept it as normal, while also refusing to treat the people in those cars as expendable.

To eliminate the friction that's grinding people down, while building systems that help them succeed in the new reality.

That's the integration. Urgency because of empathy, not despite it.

The Leaders Who Do This

The compassionate revolutionaries I've worked with share certain patterns.

They read resistance as information, not obstruction. When people push back, they look for the legitimate concern underneath the resistance rather than dismissing it or accommodating it reflexively.

They address hard realities—including headcount changes—with honesty and support rather than euphemism or avoidance.

They build coalitions before they announce transformation, because sustainable change requires willing participants, not compliant subjects.

They move fast through bureaucratic friction and slow through human transition—the opposite of what most organizations do.

They measure both velocity metrics and human metrics, tracking both cycle time reduction and psychological safety, because you need both to know if the transformation is working.

What This Creates

When you get this right, something interesting happens.

The parking lot at 5:30 PM empties—not because people care less, but because work actually works. The friction that kept them there is gone.

The organization becomes unrecognizable—not because you bulldozed the culture, but because you eliminated the scar tissue strangling it. Velocity reveals the real culture underneath the dysfunction.

People change—not because you broke them down and rebuilt them, but because they've experienced what work feels like when systems support rather than defeat them.

And you're different too. You've learned to hold complexity without being paralyzed by it. To see clearly while feeling deeply. To act decisively while remaining human.

You've become a compassionate revolutionary.

The Invitation

If you're reading this and recognizing the tension I'm describing—the way urgency and empathy pull you in different directions—you're already doing the work.

The question isn't whether the tension is real. It is.

The question is whether you'll collapse into one extreme or learn to hold both.

Whether you'll use empathy as an excuse for inaction or urgency as an excuse for callousness.

Whether you'll integrate them into something more powerful than either alone.

Urgency without empathy creates casualties.
Empathy without urgency creates stagnation.
Integration creates transformation that lasts.

The path forward isn't choosing between caring about results and caring about people.

It's caring about both with equal intensity.

That's the work. That's the calling.

That's the compassionate revolutionary.

—Johan Strömquist
