Something has been building for years. You can see it in the housing market -- median prices up sixty percent since 2019, the median household spending nearly half its income to own a median home, the median age of a first-time buyer now forty. You can see it in the wealth share of the top one percent, wider than at any point since the Federal Reserve began tracking in 1989. You can see it in the declining trust accumulated through a financial crisis, a pandemic response, a generation's worth of visible concentration at the top.

Then the narrative landscape thickened. For three years, a set of distinct alarms have been sounded, all in good faith, all landing in the same place. The CEOs of the major AI labs have told us that their technology will eliminate half of all entry-level white-collar jobs in one to five years, that unemployment could spike to ten or twenty percent, that entire categories of work will disappear. From another direction, the x-risk community has told us that AI poses an extinction-level threat comparable to nuclear weapons. Two different alarms, two different vocabularies, one combined effect: a felt trajectory of existential stakes, authored at volume, with no collective response at matching volume on the other side.

There is a research literature that predicts what happens next. It has been building patiently for decades. A 2022 paper in the Journal of Conflict Resolution, drawing on individual-level survey data across many countries, found that it is not static inequality that mobilizes people toward political violence but anticipated decline -- what Kahneman and Tversky called the domain of loss. When people believe they are sliding downward, they become risk-seeking. A systematic review of one hundred forty-one studies on radicalization and inequality points the same way: perceived socio-political inequality predicts cognitive radicalization more strongly than any objective economic measure alone. What the research has always said, in plain language, is that threats to the future do the political work that current conditions cannot do on their own.

By early 2026, the American cultural response had already begun to find its vocabulary. In late 2024, Luigi Mangione shot the CEO of UnitedHealthcare dead on a Manhattan sidewalk. He was turned into a folk hero by a substantial fraction of the people he claimed to represent -- an Emerson poll found that forty-one percent of eighteen- to twenty-nine-year-olds considered it somewhat or completely acceptable to kill a CEO. The Titan submersible implosion had been greeted with public glee. The Charlie Kirk assassination had produced debates not about whether the violence was wrong but about whether people could be fired for publicly approving it.

Into all of this came AI. Not as cause, but as the loudest new surface the pattern could find.

Four A.M. at the Gate

At four in the morning on a Friday in April, a young man threw a Molotov cocktail at Sam Altman's home. He had travelled from Texas to do it. He was arrested soon after, outside OpenAI headquarters, with kerosene and a lighter and an anti-AI manifesto written under a Dune-referencing handle. Two days later, a different pair of people fired a gun at the same house. Altman posted a photograph of his family and wrote a short, remarkable paragraph about the power of words and narratives. He said he had been awake in the middle of the night, thinking that he had underestimated that power.

The rest of the young man's profile -- unstable, radicalized, some combination -- is not the point. What is the point is what surrounded the act. The comments on the news story of the attack, thousands of likes on each. "Where can we support their bail fund?" "I hope the Molotov is okay." "Nobody should throw another Molotov cocktail at Sam Altman's house, which is pictured here in a neighborhood that would be easy to find."

That is not an outlier acting out. That is a population finding the act legible. The research literature has been saying, for as long as it has existed, that the interesting data is never the person who lit the match -- it is the crowd that warms its hands by the fire. The comment section is where the domain of loss lives.

The Library Already Written

Hindsight makes the Molotov look explainable, and it is. The explanations are not new. The research on how ordinary populations come to find political violence acceptable has been built slowly over decades, and it points with remarkable consistency at a small set of mechanisms.

Albert Bandura, writing in 1999, described the eight ways in which people disable their internal moral controls when harm serves a purpose they have accepted as good. Moral justification, euphemistic labelling, advantageous comparison, displacement and diffusion of responsibility, distortion of consequences, dehumanization, attribution of blame. The comment section was a demonstration of most of them, running in real time under a news story.

Kurt Gray and Daniel Wegner, in 2009, described moral typecasting: the way cognition sorts people into moral agents -- powerful, responsible, capable of acting on the world -- and moral patients -- recipients, vulnerable, capable of being acted upon. Once a population is typecast as patients, sympathy flows toward them; once typecast as agents, sympathy flows around them. The billionaire is an agent. The commenter is a patient. The attack becomes legible within seconds.

Jeremy Ginges and Scott Atran, in a 2007 paper in PNAS, established the backfire effect of material incentives offered against sacred concerns. When you try to settle something that people experience as identity with money, opposition intensifies rather than softens. The paper was written about religious territory, but the mechanism is more general: compensation for identity loss reads as insult.

Bo Rothstein, writing across two decades from Sweden, has documented the difference between universal and means-tested welfare: universal systems build trust across class lines; means-tested systems erode it, creating in-groups and out-groups inside what was supposed to be a shared provision.

The research is not news. It is a library, and reading with the question this moment is forcing -- how do we keep a Molotov at 4 a.m. from becoming an ordinary thing? -- the library does not offer the comfort of mystery. It points plainly toward where the focus should go.

The Narrators and the Asymmetry

The easy move from here is to blame the people who have been sounding the alarms. Dario Amodei's essays predicting white-collar displacement, Sam Altman's years of podcast appearances anticipating the obsolescence of entire job categories, the x-risk community's extinction framing, Elon Musk at Bletchley Park saying there will come a point when no job is needed. It is easy to draw a line between these narratives and the population that has come to find attacks on their authors acceptable.

The move is tempting and it is wrong.

If Amodei genuinely believes, on the evidence available to him inside his own lab, that half of entry-level white-collar jobs will disappear within five years, then saying so publicly is not incitement. It is what honest public discourse requires. He is trying to generate the political and regulatory urgency that might produce a response proportional to the stakes he sees. The question should he not say this if he believes it? does not have an answer that silences him. Assume good faith. Most of these people are sounding an alarm because they have seen enough of what is coming to be frightened by the gap between what their technology can do and what their society is prepared to absorb.

The problem is the asymmetry. The alarm has been articulated at scale, in vivid language, across years of public appearances. The answer has not. When Altman wrote at four in the morning that he had underestimated the power of words and narratives, the observation was accurate but incomplete. Words and narratives do have power. The problem is not that the narrators have authored theirs. The problem is that no one has authored an equally powerful narrative about what our society can do on the other side of the technology -- and in the absence of that second narrative, the first one is read as prophecy rather than as a call for answers.

We have the institutions we need. What we lack is vision. There is no I have a dream for the AI transition -- not one, not several, not at the level of articulation that would let our institutions act with confidence toward an end worth acting toward. The asymmetry between articulated threat and unarticulated future is where the radicalization lives.

Looking Forward

Retrospection is cheap. Given the trajectory, the research, and the Molotov, anyone can explain what happened and why. The harder discipline is to look forward with the same clarity and ask what would have to change for the next one not to happen.

The answer has two axes.

The first is sustenance. I have written about this elsewhere. A society undergoing this scale of transition needs a material floor that does not depend on the wage share of economic output, because the wage share is exactly what AI puts under pressure. The floor is affordable; the technology that is displacing the labor is also the technology that makes universal services cheaper to deliver than at any previous point in history. Universal provision of healthcare, education, basic legal guidance, housing assistance, essential utilities -- the Swedish folkhemmet, the British NHS, the Great Society were within reach in the twentieth century and are more reachable now. Those projects did not fail at providing sustenance. They failed at sustaining the political conditions under which sustenance could be guaranteed. That is a different problem, and it is tractable.

But sustenance alone is not enough. This is where the debate about universal basic income has been stuck. UBI is not wrong. The belief that UBI alone is sufficient is wrong. A check from the people who automated your labor, with no accompanying shift in your relationship to contribution and meaning, ratifies precisely the dignity inversion that the research says is most corrosive. It addresses the first axis and leaves the second untouched.

The second axis is purpose. Meaning, in its most durable definition, is the experience of believing that what we do matters -- that we have impact, that we contribute to a team, that we improve the lives of people around us. It is possible to generate purpose in solitude: a book written for no one but oneself can carry it. But purpose is overwhelmingly a social phenomenon. It arises when we participate in something larger than ourselves and see our contribution reflected back.

Participation is the conduit to purpose. No participation, no purpose. And this is what the UBI-only proposal misses: it solves the food and the rent, and it leaves the person sitting in front of a life from which the channel for meaningful participation has been removed. It is not that people want to work -- it is that people want to matter. The work was the channel through which mattering was reliably accessible.

Where Policy Starts

What would it mean to rebuild the channel? Not through nostalgia for the work that is being displaced, but through deliberate construction of new participation architecture.

It would mean public AI infrastructure -- open models, public compute, civic platforms -- treated not as consumption goods but as universal right of access to the tools. Rothstein's logic applied to the means of production: the floor must include the capability to build with the technology, not merely to receive its output.

It would mean training and use-case sharing networks inside every workplace that AI touches, which is now every workplace. This is not the co-determination of an earlier era, not veto power or brakes on adoption. AI will arrive in workplaces regardless. What is at stake is whether the people inside those workplaces arrive as participants in the adoption or as its targets. Expertise dispersion -- the same logic that turns an overwhelmed IT department into a platform for citizen developers -- is how we keep a workforce on the right side of the curve. A person who is building with AI is not a person radicalizing against it.

It would mean citizen-developer pathways that extend the logic of enablement beyond any single department: platforms through which ordinary people can assemble their own AI-augmented tools for their own contexts, without being engineers to do it. The person who builds their own workflow does not need rescuing. They have found a place where their agency still operates.

It would mean deliberative structures that give ordinary people real, consequential input into the decisions that will shape the transition. Not theatrical consultation -- citizens' assemblies on specific questions, resourced and binding. Ireland has done it on abortion. British Columbia has done it on electoral reform. Taiwan has done it on digital policy. The precedents are real. The mechanics are known.

It would mean public investment in the kinds of contribution that the market has historically under-priced: care work, education, community maintenance, arts, cultural production, civic repair. The ten thousand roles a society needs more of, not fewer, and that AI will not displace because they were never really about producing things -- they were about tending to people and the institutions that hold people together. Reskilling as redirection, in this sense, is not training for yesterday's jobs. It is actively helping people find their team -- the place where their contribution can matter to someone beyond themselves.

And it would mean a fiscal spine beneath all of this: a tax architecture that captures capital and platform returns the way twentieth-century welfare states captured wages, and treats essential utilities as infrastructure rather than commodities.

The governing line is one that applies to every technological transition we have come through. Capitalism is a machine that sorts things into efficient and inefficient. Policy is the mechanic that tells the machine what counts as valuable. AI changes the efficiency calculus. It does not change what policy can choose to value. That is still a decision. It has always been a decision.

Close

AI is not our fate. It is our material. It will not produce mass unemployment unless we allow the mechanics around it to produce mass unemployment, and it will not produce violence unless we allow the narrative around it to run without an answer. Neither is predestined. Both are shaped by what we choose to build on the other side of the capability curve.

The Molotov was a report. So was the comment section. The four thousand six hundred and thirty-one likes under I hope the Molotov is okay are a report on what happens when an alarm is sounded and no dream arrives to answer it. The research points to what common sense has always suggested: threats without channels for response become rage, and rage without direction becomes whatever the nearest symbol can absorb.

A dream is owed. Not a utopian one, not one that promises everyone a painless transition, but one that is specific enough to give our institutions a direction to act toward and inclusive enough that ordinary people can see their own contribution inside it. Dreams are what let the institutions we already possess move with confidence.

We know how to build the material floor. We know how to open channels for participation. We have a technology that makes both cheaper than at any previous moment.

What is missing is the dream. And a dream is not something we wait to receive. It is something we write.
