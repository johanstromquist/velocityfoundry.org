Operationalizing Velocity OS: From Committee Speed to Continuous Speed

By Johan Strömquist

In a previous post, we explored why biotechs run at committee speed when they could run at continuous speed. Why decisions that contain 90 minutes of actual work take 336 hours to complete. Why your parking lot is full at 9 PM not because the work is hard, but because your organization is broken.

The diagnosis was clear: Most organizations operate at 5% capacity, drowning in self-imposed friction.

Today, let's get tactical. The Velocity Operating System isn't philosophy - it's mechanics. It's built on five core components that work together to eliminate organizational friction:

The Activity Chart replaces your org chart, mapping what needs doing rather than who reports to whom. Every activity gets classified: Does it require human accountability? Human intent? Or just efficient execution? This classification determines whether humans, AI agents, or automation should handle it.

The Number gives everyone a single velocity metric they own. Not a scorecard, not KPIs - one number that answers whether they're accelerating or braking the organization. When it's green, they have full autonomy. When it's red, they need support or replacement.

Rocks are 90-day velocity sprints - concrete outcomes that prove the model works. If it takes longer than 90 days, it's not a Rock, it's a wish. Each Rock must measurably increase organizational speed.

The Solve Forever Protocol (SFP) ensures problems get eliminated, not managed. See it through transparency, solve it through elimination or automation, systematize it so it never recurs. If you're solving the same problem twice, you failed the first time.

Alignment Pulse is your weekly 45-minute synchronization - not a decision meeting, but a system adjustment meeting. Decisions about work flow automatically. Decisions about the system happen here.

These aren't independent tools. They form an integrated operating system where decision authority flows to data, not up hierarchies. Where problems get solved permanently, not perpetually managed. Where the organization's clock speed increases without anyone working harder.

Fair warning: This will reduce the number required to achieve the same outcomes. Not as a goal, but as physics. When coordination gets automated and approval hierarchies disappear, the roles that existed to manage them disappear too. You can use this efficiency to expand or optimize, but you can't pretend it won't happen.

Let's walk through how to actually build this.

Start With Your Activity Chart

Forget your org chart. It maps people, not purpose. The Activity Chart maps every significant activity in your organization and asks three questions:

  • Does this activity require direct human accountability - regulatory sign-offs, strategic commitments, outcomes someone must own?
  • Does it require human intent - setting goals, defining success, expressing what matters?
  • Or is it just execution - analysis, processing, coordination - where the only metric is efficiency?

Map your top 20 activities this way. You'll discover something uncomfortable: Most of what your organization does falls into that third category. Most of your headcount is executing tasks that don't require human intent or accountability. They're human middleware.

Any activity that does not explicitly require human accountability or creative intent is a candidate for an AI agent, or full-blown automation.

This isn't judgment. It's physics. And physics doesn't care about your feelings.

Assign Every Person One Number

Not a balanced scorecard. Not a KPI dashboard. One number that captures their contribution to organizational velocity.

When someone asks "What's your number?", they should immediately know three things:

  • What they're accountable for,
  • What good looks like, and
  • Where they are now.

A clinical operations manager might own "protocol amendments processed per week." Target: 5. Current: 2. The gap is clear. The accountability is clear. The path forward is clear.

This number isn't for performance theater. It's for decision authority. Green number? Full autonomy. Red number? You either get support, get moved, or get gone. No ambiguity, no politics, no committee needed to interpret performance.

The power comes from the question itself. "What's your number?" becomes organizational language for "Are you accelerating or braking?"

Just remember that when circumstances change, so must the number.

Replace Committees with Criteria

Every committee meeting is a confession that you haven't defined decision criteria. Budget committees exist because you haven't pre-defined spending authority. Protocol committees exist because you haven't specified modification triggers. Review committees exist because you haven't automated quality thresholds.

Take your five most frequent committee meetings. Document what they actually decide. Now pre-define the criteria: Below $50K? Automatic approval. Enrollment below 80% at week 12? Trigger site expansion. Deviation rate above 5%? Initiate retraining.

The person closest to the data makes the decision within these guardrails. The committee becomes a quarterly review to adjust criteria, not a weekly bottleneck to interpret them.

Make Meetings About the System, Not the Work

Your weekly alignment meeting runs 45 minutes maximum. Five minutes for everyone to state their number. Ten minutes for Rock progress - on track, off track, or blocked. Twenty-five minutes solving the biggest constraint using the Solve Forever Protocol. Five minutes for system decisions - changes to criteria, delegation, or structure.

These meetings can make decisions. They decide to change decision criteria. They allocate resources. They adjust priorities. What they don't do is become the waiting room for operational approvals that should flow automatically.

A protocol amendment doesn't need a meeting. It needs pre-defined criteria and someone with authority at the point of data. A budget approval doesn't need committee debate. It needs thresholds and automatic routing.

Implement the Solve Forever Protocol

When problems surface - and they will - resist the muscle memory of management. Don't create a tracking spreadsheet. Don't assign a coordinator. Don't schedule a recurring meeting.

Instead: See it, Solve it, Systematize it.

See it through transparency, not through meetings. Problems should surface from data flows, not status reports. Solve it by elimination first, automation second, delegation third - never by adding human oversight. Systematize it into code, platform, or process that handles every future instance.

If you're solving the same problem twice, you failed the first time. If you're managing a problem for more than 90 days, you're not solving it, you're institutionalizing it.

Build Your First Rock

Pick something that takes two weeks today and make it take two days. Not by working harder or adding people, but by removing the waiting.

Map where those two weeks actually go. You'll find 90 minutes of real work and 13.5 days of sitting in queues, waiting for meetings, seeking approvals, clarifying requirements. Attack the waiting, not the work.

This becomes your first 90-day Rock. Concrete, measurable, achievable without heroics. When you deliver a 90% reduction in cycle time for one critical process, you've proved the model. The second Rock gets easier. The third becomes inevitable.

Face the Headcount Reality

Implementing Velocity OS will reduce headcount. Not as a goal, but as a consequence. When information flows transparently, information brokers become redundant. When decisions flow to data, decision interpreters become unnecessary. When AI handles execution, human executors need new roles or new employers.

You can use this efficiency gain two ways: Expand capabilities with the same resources, or maintain output with fewer resources. Both are valid strategies. But pretending you'll implement Velocity OS while keeping everyone in their current roles is delusional.

The choice isn't whether people will be displaced. It's whether you'll manage that displacement thoughtfully or let it happen chaotically.

The Infrastructure Investment

You can't run at speed on email and Excel. You need systems that capture data at the source, flow it transparently, and trigger decisions automatically. This doesn't mean a three-year ERP implementation. It means rapid deployment of focused tools, often built by your own teams using AI assistance.

GSK discovered they could run queries in 30 minutes that previously took a year - not through massive infrastructure projects, but through targeted automation of specific bottlenecks. Moderna designed their COVID vaccine in 48 hours because they'd spent a decade building a platform where designing vaccines was like updating code.

The infrastructure you need probably isn't the infrastructure you think you need. You need decision flow, not dashboard proliferation.

The 90-Day Proof

In 90 days, you can prove Velocity OS works. Pick one department. Map their activities. Assign numbers. Define their Rock. Pre-define decision criteria. Implement weekly alignment. Build one piece of automation.

Watch what happens. Cycle times drop without anyone working harder. Decisions that took weeks take hours. The parking lot starts emptying at reasonable hours because work flows instead of pooling.

Most importantly, that department stops being the bottleneck for everyone else. Their velocity increase cascades through the organization. Other departments start asking what changed. The transformation becomes pull, not push.

The Uncomfortable Truth

Your organization is running at a fraction of its potential speed. Not because your people aren't talented or dedicated - they are. But because your operating system was designed for a world where humans were the only option for executing work, where information was scarce rather than abundant, where coordination required meetings rather than systems.

That world no longer exists. The question isn't whether you need Velocity OS. It's whether you'll implement it by choice, have it forced on you by competitors who already have, or find yourself without a company to implement it in.

The operating system can be upgraded. But first, you have to admit your current one is broken. Then you have to decide: Will you be the organization that moves at committee speed while others move at the speed of data? Or will you build the infrastructure, governance, and culture that operates at the natural speed of your science?

Start Monday. Pick your worst bottleneck. Map the waiting. Remove it.

In 90 days, you won't recognize your own velocity.

Ready to operationalize velocity in your organization? The framework is here. The tools exist. The only question is whether you'll move fast enough to matter.
